Notes

The starting point of this project was a challenge initiated by the Data Viz society to create a data visualization for Earth Day 2020. Being someone who loves cooking and even more eating, visualizing the environmental impact of our daily food choices was the natural way to go. On top of that, my friends often ask me where I find inspiration for plant-based meals. So I thought it would be a good occasion to go public with my obsession with the recipes from My New Roots and Pick Up Limes and how their recipes never fail at making me want to get lost in my kitchen.

Most of us eat 3 to 5 times Every. Single. Day. What we choose to eat is one of the most efficient ways to help our planet and making a huge difference doesn’t necessarily require to go full-time vegan. Small changes, applied regularly, can have a MASSIVE impact when cumulated over the course of one year. My hope for this visualization is to help you get a better feel of the repercussions these small changes can make and gather a few ideas for food swaps and new recipes to try out. You’re welcome!

Methodology

This visualization is based on the data collected by Hannah Ritchie and Max Roser in the article Environmental impacts of food production. By combining food products from this collection, I created meals and listed possible product and recipe swaps.

For each meal and swap, the the overall foodprint is calculated. This foodprint is a combination of 4 factors: the Land use (m2), the Greenhouse gas emissions (kgCO2eq), the Water use (L) and the Eutrophying emissions (kgPO4eq).

Since some ingredients can increase some of the factors while reducing others, I came up with a Foodprint index to help better assess how the choices made within this visualization increase or decrease our environmental impact. This factor represents how big the impact of our choices is compared to the maximal impact that can be reached within this visualization, on a scale from 0 to 20.

Assessing the foodprint of a product is very complex. The evaluation methods and impact factors vary from one study to the next which makes them complex to compare. This is why I decided to stick to the collection of data made available by Hannah Ritchie and Max Roser1.

For the same reasons, I limited the visualization to the main ingredients of each recipe and had to perform a few approximation, for example using the data on tofu for edamame and adjusting the calculation to the amount required for a portion of each.

Bottomline, the numbers generated by this visualization are here to give a global overview on how our food choices impact our environment, and not to provide precise numbers.

In the future, it would be interesting to add more day-to-day ingredients to the meals and swap choices, like yogurt and butter for example. This would make this visualization even more applicable to our daily life.
